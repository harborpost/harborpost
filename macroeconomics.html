Unit 1 — Basic Economic Concepts

Scarcity forces choices. Choices create costs. Good models help us see trade-offs.

Core Definitions

  • Scarcity: Limited resources vs unlimited wants.
  • Trade-off: Choosing more of one thing means less of another.
  • Opportunity Cost: Value of the best alternative forgone.
  • Marginal Analysis: Compare marginal benefit (MB) with marginal cost (MC); choose where MB ≥ MC.

Key Relationships

  • Net Benefit = Total Benefit − Total Cost
  • Optimal Quantity occurs near MB = MC.
  • Opp. Cost of X = Loss of Y / Gain of X (from a trade-off table).

Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

  • Points on the curve: efficient; inside: inefficient; outside: unattainable (without growth).
  • Bowed-out PPC: increasing opportunity cost; straight PPC: constant opportunity cost.
  • Growth shifts the entire PPC outward (more resources, better technology, human capital).

Worked Example — Opportunity Cost

A worker can produce either 10 loaves of bread or 5 shirts per day. The opportunity cost of 1 shirt is 2 loaves (10/5). The opportunity cost of 1 loaf is 0.5 shirt.

Use: These ratios drive comparative advantage and terms of trade.

Comparative vs. Absolute Advantage

  • Absolute advantage: Produce more with the same inputs.
  • Comparative advantage: Lower opportunity cost — basis for specialization and gains from trade.
  • Terms of Trade: Acceptable price range lies between each party’s opportunity cost.

Takeaways

  • Scarcity → choices → opportunity costs. Always think at the margin.
  • PPC shows efficiency and growth; trade can allow consumption beyond a country’s PPC.
  • Comparative advantage (not absolute) determines who should specialize in what.

Unit 2 — Economic Indicators & the Business Cycle

How we measure the economy: output, prices, and labor markets — and how they move over time.

GDP Basics

  • GDP: Market value of all final goods & services produced within a country in a given period.
  • Excludes: intermediate goods, used goods, purely financial transactions, household production, illegal markets (usually).
  • Expenditure approach: Y = C + I + G + (X − M).
  • Income approach: Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit (should ≈ expenditure approach).

Real vs. Nominal & Price Indices

  • Nominal GDP: Output valued at current prices.
  • Real GDP: Output valued at base-year prices.
  • GDP Deflator: Deflator = (Nominal / Real) × 100
  • Inflation (via CPI): π ≈ (CPIₜ − CPIₜ₋₁)/CPIₜ₋₁ × 100
  • Real interest rate: r ≈ i − π

Worked Example — Real vs. Nominal GDP

Suppose Nominal GDP = 1,100 and GDP Deflator = 110. Then Real GDP = 1,100 / 1.10 = 1,000.

If next year Real GDP rises to 1,030, real growth ≈ 3%.

Common Pitfalls

  • CPI overstates inflation due to substitution and new-goods bias.
  • Nominal wage increases can still mean lower real wages if inflation is higher.

Unemployment

  • Labor Force: Employed + Unemployed (actively seeking).
  • Unemployment rate: U / Labor Force × 100
  • Types: frictional (search), structural (skills mismatch), cyclical (business cycle), seasonal.
  • Natural rate (NRU): Frictional + Structural (when output at potential).

Business Cycle & Output Gaps

  • Phases: expansion → peak → contraction → trough.
  • Recessionary gap: Actual output < potential (higher cyclical unemployment).
  • Inflationary gap: Actual output > potential (low unemployment, rising inflation pressure).

Takeaways

  • Use Real GDP to compare output; use CPI/Deflator to track prices.
  • Inflation distorts nominal values — convert to real.
  • NRU exists even at potential output; cyclical unemployment indicates gaps.

Unit 3 — National Income & Price Determination (AD–AS)

Short-run fluctuations, long-run potential, and how the economy self-corrects.

Aggregate Demand (AD)

  • AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • Downward sloping due to wealth effect, interest-rate effect, and net export effect.
  • Shifters: changes in C, I, G, NX (consumer confidence, taxes, investment expectations, foreign income, exchange rates).

Aggregate Supply (AS)

  • SRAS: Upward sloping (sticky wages/prices). Shifts with input costs (wages, commodities), productivity, taxes/regulation, expectations.
  • LRAS: Vertical at potential output (full employment). Shifts with resources, technology, institutions, human capital.

Equilibrium & Gaps

  • Short-run equilibrium at AD ∩ SRAS; price level and output determined there.
  • Long-run equilibrium when AD ∩ SRAS ∩ LRAS.
  • Recessionary gap: AD intersects SRAS left of LRAS → unemployment > NRU.
  • Inflationary gap: AD intersects SRAS right of LRAS → unemployment < NRU.

Self-correction: In a recession, wages fall → SRAS shifts right toward LRAS. In an inflation, wages rise → SRAS shifts left.

Multipliers

  • Spending multiplier: 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS
  • Tax multiplier: − MPC / MPS
  • Balanced budget multiplier ≈ 1

Worked Example — Closing a Recessionary Gap

Output gap = $200b; MPC = 0.8 → spending multiplier = 1 / 0.2 = 5. A $40b ↑ in government spending could close the gap (40 × 5 = 200).

Using a tax cut instead: tax multiplier = −0.8 / 0.2 = −4; need a $50b tax cut (50 × 4 = 200).

Watch Out

  • Shifts vs. movements along AD/AS — rate changes cause movement along AD via interest-rate effect, not an AD shift.
  • Supply shocks (oil) shift SRAS, not AD.

Takeaways

  • AD changes drive short-run output; SRAS changes drive cost-push inflation.
  • Long-run output is set by LRAS (resources & productivity), not by the price level.
  • Multipliers amplify fiscal changes; size depends on MPC/MPS.

Unit 4 — Financial Sector

Money, banks, interest rates, and how policy moves from reserves to the real economy.

Money & Banking

  • Functions: medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value.
  • Types: commodity, fiat. M1 (currency + checkable deposits), M2 (M1 + savings, small time deposits, money market funds).
  • Fractional reserve banking: banks keep required reserves (rr) and loan the rest.

Money Creation

  • Simple money multiplier ≈ 1 / rr (upper bound; leakages reduce it).
  • Excess reserves = Actual reserves − Required reserves.

Money Market

  • Vertical money supply (set by central bank) vs. downward-sloping money demand.
  • Open market purchase → MS shifts right → lower nominal i → ↑ investment → ↑ AD.
  • Open market sale → MS left → higher i → ↓ I → ↓ AD.

Loanable Funds Market

  • Real interest rate on y-axis; supply = national saving; demand = investment (plus gov’t deficits).
  • ↑ budget deficit → demand for funds ↑ (or supply of national saving ↓) → rreal ↑ → crowding out of private investment.
  • ↑ capital inflows from abroad → supply of funds ↑ → rreal ↓ → investment ↑.

Monetary Policy Tools

  • Open market operations (buy/sell bonds).
  • Reserve requirement (rarely used for fine-tuning).
  • Discount rate & interest on reserves.

Worked Example — T-Account

Bank has $100 in deposits; rr = 10% → required reserves = $10. If reserves = $20, excess reserves = $10; new loans up to $10. With a simple multiplier of 10, maximum potential money creation = $100 (if fully lent & re-deposited with no leakages).

Takeaways

  • Money market sets nominal i in the short run; loanable funds sets rreal via saving & investment.
  • Open market purchases lower i → stimulate investment and AD; sales do the opposite.
  • Deficits can crowd out investment by raising real interest rates.

Unit 5 — Stabilization Policies & Long-Run Consequences

How fiscal and monetary tools affect inflation, unemployment, and growth — in the short and long run.

Fiscal Policy

  • Expansionary: ↑ G or ↓ T to close recessionary gaps (AD →).
  • Contractionary: ↓ G or ↑ T to close inflationary gaps (AD ←).
  • Automatic stabilizers: taxes & transfers that vary with income (progressive tax, unemployment benefits) — reduce volatility without new laws.
  • Lags: recognition, decision, implementation.

Monetary Policy

  • Expansionary: buy bonds → MS ↑ → i ↓ → I ↑ → AD →
  • Contractionary: sell bonds → MS ↓ → i ↑ → I ↓ → AD ←
  • Strengths: speed & flexibility; Limits: liquidity traps, expectations, weak credit demand.

Phillips Curve

  • SRPC: inverse short-run relationship between inflation and unemployment.
  • LRPC: vertical at NRU; no long-run trade-off.
  • Expected inflation shifts SRPC: if expectations rise, SRPC shifts up/right.

Worked Link — AD–AS and Phillips

AD → (expansion) raises price level and output → unemployment falls (move down along SRPC); persistent demand-pull inflation can shift expectations → SRPC shifts up (long-run unemployment returns to NRU).

Growth & LRAS

  • LRAS shifts right with higher productivity, capital formation, technology, and labor force growth.
  • Policy levers: education/human capital, infrastructure, R&D, institutions & rule of law.

Budget Deficits, Debt, & Crowding Out

  • Deficits add to public debt; sustained deficits can raise rreal and crowd out private investment.
  • But during recessions, well-targeted deficits may raise future growth if they fund productive investment.

Takeaways

  • Short-run trade-off (SRPC) disappears in the long run (LRPC at NRU).
  • Use fiscal/monetary to close gaps, but watch lags, expectations, and crowding out.
  • Long-run growth is supply-side: shift LRAS via productivity and capital.

Unit 6 — Open Economy: International Trade & Finance

How goods, services, and capital cross borders — and how exchange rates connect everything.

Balance of Payments (BOP)

  • Current account: goods/services trade (NX), income flows, transfers.
  • Financial (capital) account: asset transactions (bonds, stocks, real assets).
  • They sum to ~0 (ignoring statistical discrepancy): a current account deficit is offset by a financial account surplus.

Foreign Exchange (FOREX)

  • Price of a currency (e.g., USD/JPY) set by supply and demand.
  • Drivers of demand for a currency: foreign purchases of that country’s goods/assets, higher relative interest rates, relatively lower inflation, expected appreciation.
  • Appreciation makes exports dearer, imports cheaper → NX falls; depreciation does the opposite.

Policy & Exchange Rates

  • Expansionary fiscal (closed economy: AD →) can raise rreal (via deficits) → foreign capital inflows → currency appreciates → NX ↓ (net export effect dampens AD).
  • Expansionary monetary lowers i → currency depreciates → NX ↑ → reinforces AD.
  • Fixed exchange rate regimes require central bank intervention (buy/sell currency) and adequate reserves.

Worked Example — FOREX Shift

If domestic interest rates rise relative to abroad, foreigners demand more domestic bonds → demand for domestic currency ↑ → appreciation. Expect NX to fall as exports become relatively more expensive.

Trade Policies

  • Tariffs: raise domestic prices, reduce imports, generate tariff revenue, create deadweight loss.
  • Quotas: limit quantity; similar welfare effects without revenue (unless quota licenses are sold).
  • Protection helps some producers but reduces total surplus.

Watch Out

  • “Strong currency is always good” — not true: it hurts exporters.
  • “Trade deficit means weak economy” — not necessarily; often linked to strong capital inflows.

Takeaways

  • BOP links trade flows with capital flows; they offset.
  • Exchange rates respond to interest rates, inflation, income, and expectations.
  • Open-economy effects can reinforce or weaken domestic policy impacts on AD.
