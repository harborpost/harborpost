Unit 1 — Basic Economic Concepts

Scarcity → choices → trade-offs → opportunity cost. How individuals, firms, and societies allocate limited resources. Models that simplify decision-making and show gains from specialization & trade.

Big Ideas
  • Every choice has a cost (the next best alternative forgone).
  • Rational decision makers think at the margin: compare marginal benefit (MB) to marginal cost (MC).
  • Specialization according to comparative advantage raises total output and enables mutually beneficial trade.
  • Models (PPC, circular flow) help analyze trade-offs, efficiency, growth, and interactions among sectors.

1) Scarcity, Choice, and Trade-offs

Resources are finite; wants are effectively unlimited. Scarcity forces choice.

  • Scarce resources (“factors of production”)
    • Land: natural resources (land, water, minerals, timber).
    • Labor: human effort, time, skills; measured in hours/quality of work.
    • Capital:
      • Physical: tools, machines, factories that raise productivity.
      • Human: education, training, experience (skills embedded in workers).
    • Entrepreneurship: risk-taking + organization to combine inputs in new ways.
  • Choice & trade-offs
    • Choosing more of Good A means giving up some of Good B because inputs are limited.
    • Trade-off ≠ opportunity cost: a choice has many trade-offs; opportunity cost is the single highest-valued alternative forgone.
  • Opportunity cost (OC)
    • Measures value of the next best option, not every alternative.
    • Includes implicit costs (time, foregone salary) and explicit costs (money outlays).
    • Example: Attending a concert costs $60 (ticket) and 3 hours you could work for $20/hr ⇒ OC = $60 + $60 = $120.
  • Rational behavior & marginal analysis
    • Rational agents continue an activity if MB ≥ MC and stop when MB < MC.
    • Net benefit = Total Benefit − Total Cost; choose option with highest expected net benefit.
    • Sunk cost is irrelevant to marginal decisions (don’t “throw good money after bad”).

2) Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

Shows maximum attainable combinations of two goods given current resources and technology.

  • Reading the PPC
    • Points on the curve: productive efficiency (all resources fully employed).
    • Points inside: inefficient (unemployment, idle capacity, misallocation).
    • Points outside: currently unattainable (require growth or trade).
  • Shape
    • Bowed-out (concave) PPC: increasing OC — resources specialized; as you produce more of Good A, you use resources less suited to A ⇒ OC rises.
    • Linear PPC: constant OC — resources are perfectly adaptable between goods.
  • Opportunity cost on the PPC
    • Moving from one point to another reveals the quantity of the other good given up.
    • Compute OC as ΔY / ΔX (units of Y forgone per extra X).
  • Economic growth & shifts
    • Outward shift: ↑ resources (labor force, capital), ↑ technology, ↑ human capital, institutional improvements.
    • Inward shift: resource destruction (natural disasters, war), institutional collapse.
    • Biased growth: tech improvement in one sector rotates the PPC outward more for that good.
  • Unemployment vs. underemployment on the PPC
    • Both show up as points inside the curve; moving to the curve increases real output without growth.

3) Specialization, Comparative Advantage, and Terms of Trade

Trade lets each party consume beyond its PPC if each specializes in what it produces at lower OC.

  • Absolute vs. comparative advantage
    • Absolute advantage: produce more with the same inputs (or the same with fewer inputs).
    • Comparative advantage: produce at lower opportunity cost — the key driver of trade gains.
    • Even if one party has absolute advantage in both goods, trade can be beneficial if OCs differ.
  • Finding opportunity costs
    • In an output table (e.g., tons of wheat or steel): OC of Wheat = Steelforgone / Wheatgained.
    • In an input table (e.g., hours per unit): invert logic; lower input requirement ⇒ absolute advantage; for comparative advantage, compute per-unit input OCs.
  • Specialization rule
    • Each country (or person) specializes in the good where its OC is lower.
    • Specialization increases total world output (expands joint PPC).
  • Terms of Trade (ToT)
    • Trade is mutually beneficial when the ToT (the “price” of one good in units of the other) lies between each party’s OC for that good.
    • Example: If OCA(1 wheat) = 0.5 steel and OCB(1 wheat) = 2 steel, a ToT of 1 wheat for 1 steel benefits both (0.5 < 1 < 2).
  • Gains from trade
    • Measured by consumption beyond the autarky (no-trade) consumption point.
    • Graphically: consumption point outside an individual PPC (with trade) vs. on/inside without trade.
  • Common pitfalls
    • Confusing absolute with comparative advantage.
    • Setting ToT outside the OC interval (one side would reject).
    • Mixing up input vs. output tables when computing OCs.

4) Economic Systems & Incentives

How societies answer the “what, how, for whom” questions.

  • Market (capitalist) system
    • Decentralized decisions by households and firms; prices convey information and incentives.
    • Private property rights, voluntary exchange, competition.
    • Strengths: efficiency, innovation, consumer choice. Weaknesses: inequality, market failures (externalities, public goods), business cycles.
  • Command (planned) system
    • Central authority sets output, prices, and allocation.
    • Strengths: can mobilize resources quickly for priorities. Weaknesses: information problems, weak incentives ⇒ inefficiency/shortages.
  • Mixed economies (most real-world systems)
    • Markets allocate most resources; government corrects market failures, provides public goods, redistributes income, stabilizes economy.
    • Extent of government role varies by country and over time.
  • Role of institutions & incentives
    • Well-defined property rights encourage investment and exchange.
    • Rule of law lowers transaction costs and uncertainty; corruption does the opposite.
    • Incentives shape behavior: taxes/subsidies, regulation, social norms.

5) Economic Goals: Efficiency, Equity, Growth, Stability, Freedom

Societies balance multiple objectives; policy often trades one goal for another.

  • Efficiency
    • Productive efficiency: produce at lowest possible cost (on PPC; in firms, at min ATC in long run).
    • Allocative efficiency: produce the mix of goods most valued by society; where MB = MC.
  • Equity
    • Fairness of income/wealth distribution; may conflict with efficiency.
    • Tools: progressive taxation, transfers, public services.
  • Growth
    • ↑ productive capacity over time (outward shift of PPC; ↑ real GDP per capita).
    • Drivers: capital accumulation, tech progress, human capital, institutions, openness to trade.
  • Stability
    • Low and predictable inflation; low cyclical unemployment; stable financial system.
    • Macroeconomic stabilization uses fiscal and monetary policy (covered in later units).
  • Economic freedom
    • Ability to choose jobs, start businesses, consume desired goods, and trade.
  • Trade-offs across goals
    • Equity vs. efficiency; short-run stabilization vs. long-run growth; environmental protection vs. output today.

6) Positive vs. Normative Economics

Separate testable statements from value-based prescriptions.

  • Positive statements
    • Descriptive; can be proven true/false with evidence (e.g., “A rent ceiling below equilibrium creates shortages”).
  • Normative statements
    • Value judgments about what should be (e.g., “The minimum wage ought to be increased”).
    • Cannot be falsified purely by data; depend on societal preferences.
  • Why the distinction matters
    • Policy debates mix empirical predictions (positive) with goals (normative).
    • Clarity prevents talking past one another and helps evaluate evidence.

7) Circular Flow of Income & Expenditure

Tracks how money, goods/services, and resources move between sectors.

  • Basic two-sector model (households & firms)
    • Product market: firms sell goods/services; households buy.
    • Factor market: households sell resources (labor, land, capital); firms buy and pay incomes (wages, rent, interest, profit).
  • Expanded model
    • Government: collects taxes; buys goods/services; makes transfers.
    • Financial sector: intermediates saving into investment (loanable funds); determines interest rates.
    • Rest of world: exports (X) and imports (M); capital flows (covered in open-economy unit).
  • Leakages & injections
    • Leakages: saving, taxes, imports.
    • Injections: investment, government purchases, exports.
    • In equilibrium (simplified): Leakages = Injections.

8) Price System Basics: Demand, Supply, and Market Coordination

How prices guide allocation; deep elasticity/market structures come later, but the foundations belong here.

  • Demand
    • Law of demand: holding other things constant, as price ↑, quantity demanded ↓.
    • Shifters (non-price):
      • Income (normal vs inferior goods)
      • Prices of related goods (substitutes, complements)
      • Tastes/preferences
      • Expectations (future prices/income)
      • Number of buyers (market size)
  • Supply
    • Law of supply: holding other things constant, as price ↑, quantity supplied ↑.
    • Shifters (non-price):
      • Input costs
      • Technology/productivity
      • Taxes & subsidies
      • Producer expectations
      • Number of sellers
  • Equilibrium
    • Where Qd = Qs; the market-clearing price and quantity.
    • Surplus: price above equilibrium; downward pressure on price.
    • Shortage: price below equilibrium; upward pressure on price.
  • Policy wedges (brief preview)
    • Price ceiling (binding) ⇒ shortage; non-price rationing; DWL.
    • Price floor (binding) ⇒ surplus; storage or wasted resources; DWL.
    • Tax wedge ⇒ buyers pay more, sellers receive less; incidence depends on elasticities.

9) Costs, Benefits, and Decision Rules

Micro logic you’ll reuse across macro topics (investment, labor, policy evaluation).

  • Accounting vs. economic profit
    • Accounting profit = Total revenue − explicit costs.
    • Economic profit = Total revenue − (explicit + implicit costs).
  • Marginal decision rule
    • Choose the level of activity where MB = MC (or last unit with MB ≥ MC).
    • In investment decisions, compare present values (time value of money in later units).
  • Common fallacies
    • Sunk cost fallacy: considering past, unrecoverable costs in current decisions.
    • Post hoc fallacy: assuming causation from mere sequence.
    • Composition fallacy: what’s true for a part isn’t necessarily true for the whole (e.g., paradox of thrift).

10) Productivity & Growth (Primer for Later Units)

Foundational links between inputs, technology, and living standards.

  • Labor productivity
    • Output per worker (or per hour); driven by capital deepening, human capital, technology, and institutions.
    • Higher productivity ⇒ higher real wages over time.
  • Capital vs. consumer goods trade-off
    • Investing more in capital goods today (sacrificing consumption) can yield faster growth (larger PPC in future).
  • Human capital & externalities
    • Education and health raise worker effectiveness; positive spillovers justify public support.

11) Key Vocabulary & Notation (Used Throughout Course)

  • Real vs. nominal: real adjusts for inflation; nominal is current dollars.
  • Stocks vs. flows: stock is a quantity at a point in time (wealth); flow is over time (income, saving).
  • Ceteris paribus: “holding other things constant,” used to isolate causal effects.
  • Exogenous vs. endogenous: given vs. determined inside a model.
  • Short run vs. long run: in macro, long run is when wages/prices fully adjust; in micro, long run is when all inputs are variable.
  • MB, MC: marginal benefit/cost.
  • ATC, AVC, AFC: average total/variable/fixed cost (micro, later use).
  • LRAS, SRAS, AD: long-run/short-run aggregate supply & aggregate demand (later units).
  • X, M, NX: exports, imports, net exports.
  • π: inflation rate (context-dependent).

12) Worked Examples (Text-Only)

  • OC from PPC points
    • Suppose moving from A→B increases Good A by +10 but reduces Good B by −30 ⇒ OC(ΔA=10) = 30 ⇒ OC of 1 A = 3 B.
    • Increasing OC: as you move further toward A, each additional A costs more B (resources less suited to A are pulled in).
  • Comparative advantage with outputs
    • Country 1: 20 wheat or 10 steel; Country 2: 12 wheat or 6 steel.
    • OC1(1 wheat) = 0.5 steel; OC2(1 wheat) = 0.5 steel ⇒ same OC ⇒ no gains from trade purely on CA; need different OCs for mutually beneficial trade.
    • Change: Country 2 becomes 12 wheat or 4 steel ⇒ OC2(1 wheat) = 0.33 steel; country 2 has CA in wheat, country 1 in steel.
  • Terms of trade
    • If OC1(1 steel) = 2 wheat and OC2(1 steel) = 3 wheat, any ToT where 2 < ToT(steel in wheat) < 3 benefits both (e.g., 1 steel for 2.5 wheat).
  • Demand/Supply shock intuition
    • Good weather raises crop yields ⇒ supply shifts right ⇒ price ↓, quantity ↑.
    • Higher consumer income for a normal good ⇒ demand shifts right ⇒ price ↑, quantity ↑.

13) Common Mistakes & Quick Fixes

  • Using money price changes to claim “demand changed” — a price change moves along the curve; demand shifts need a non-price determinant.
  • Swapping trade-offs with opportunity cost — OC is the single best alternative forgone.
  • Confusing productive with allocative efficiency — productive is “lowest cost”; allocative is “MB = MC.”
  • Setting terms of trade outside the OC interval — one side will refuse, so no mutual gain.
  • Treating sunk costs as relevant — ignore them; focus on MB and MC going forward.

14) Bridges to Later Units

  • From PPC to AD-AS: Growth (outward PPC) parallels rightward LRAS shifts; negative supply shocks resemble inward capacity moves.
  • From specialization to trade balance: Comparative advantage underpins patterns of exports/imports and affects AD via NX.
  • From marginal analysis to policy: Cost-benefit logic frames fiscal project selection and regulation design.

15) Mini-Glossary (Rapid Review)

  • Scarcity: limited resources, unlimited wants.
  • Opportunity cost: value of next best alternative forgone.
  • Trade-off: giving up one thing to get another.
  • Marginal: relating to an additional unit.
  • Comparative advantage: lower opportunity cost.
  • Absolute advantage: higher productivity.
  • Terms of trade: rate of exchange in trade.
  • Productive efficiency: on PPC (lowest cost).
  • Allocative efficiency: MB = MC (right mix).
  • Circular flow: money, goods/services, and resources moving between sectors.
